History of Scouting

From attics to online archives: Scout leaders built a 13,500-item virtual museum

By Darrin Scheid • March 3, 2026
ADVERTISEMENT
(300 x 600)

Scouting America historians Andrew Coe and Steve Miller are attempting to collect every staff list from the past 97 seasons at Camp Horseshoe, a Scouting America camp in Rising Sun, Maryland.

They’ll be added to thousands of items already collected for the Horseshoe Scout Reservation Alumni Association (HSRAA) virtual museum. “Opened” in 2008, the museum houses one of the largest collections of Scouting memorabilia on the East Coast.

How to Contribute

More than 150 people have contributed to the collection. Some sent a single photo; others mailed entire boxes of slides and memorabilia. Items are carefully photographed or scanned, then returned if possible, ensuring families retain their keepsakes while sharing them with the broader Scouting community.

They started the project to provide a central location for a few items. Now with about 13,500 photographs, staff rosters, newsletters, pictures and cups and more, Coe and Miller hope the collection inspires others to preserve the history of their camps and troops.

As Coe and Miller have discovered, persistence and a passion for the work often pays off. The duo used that persistence and passion when they first started the museum with about 2,000 pieces, collected the way you might assume — by asking around. They reached out to alumni, former camp staff members and longtime Scout leaders to contribute items tucked away in shoeboxes.

As technology evolved and the collection grew, the team knew they needed a more sustainable solution. They eventually migrated the museum to WordPress, a content management system that made the site more mobile-friendly and easier to maintain. The shift also ensured that future volunteers wouldn’t need advanced coding skills to keep the museum alive.

ADVERTISEMENT (300 x 250)

What makes the HSRAA virtual museum even more interesting is an emphasis on storytelling. Some exhibits feature video interviews with longtime Scouters sharing memories. That focus on tradition includes documenting Camp Horseshoe’s nightly retreat ceremony — a marching program dating back to the 1940s — complete with original instructions, photos and decades of trophy designs.

In addition to all those staff lists, Coe and Miller are also on the lookout for someone to carry on the work. They need somebody with a passion for history and some web content management skills.

“We have staff members now who constantly send me items from the past,” Coe says.
